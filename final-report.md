# CalmCampus: Designing and Evaluating a Student Mental Health Support App

## 1. Project Overview

**Project theme:** Health & Well-being  
**Project type:** Mobile-first digital interface prototype  
**Target users:** University students aged 18-26 who experience stress, anxiety, loneliness, academic pressure, or difficulty accessing counseling support.  
**Group size:** 4-6 members  
**Project duration:** 6 weeks plus final presentation

CalmCampus is a student mental health support app designed to help university students check their emotional state, access quick self-care resources, book counselor appointments, and receive anonymous peer support in a safe and simple interface.

The project follows user-centered design principles: understanding user needs, creating personas, sketching and prototyping solutions, testing usability with users, refining the design, and presenting the final interface.

## 2. Problem Statement

Many university students experience stress, anxiety, academic pressure, financial worries, relationship issues, and loneliness. Although counseling services may exist on campus, students often do not know how to access them, fear stigma, or delay seeking help until the situation becomes serious.

Current support channels are often fragmented: notices on boards, office visits, phone calls, informal peer advice, or social media groups. These channels can be hard to navigate, especially when a student is emotionally overwhelmed.

**Problem statement:** University students need a private, accessible, and easy-to-use digital interface that helps them understand their mood, find immediate self-care support, and connect to professional or peer help without confusion or fear of judgment.

## 3. Project Objectives

1. Design a usable student mental health support interface based on real user needs.
2. Help students perform four main tasks: mood check-in, self-care, counselor booking, and anonymous support chat.
3. Apply HCI principles such as visibility, feedback, consistency, accessibility, error prevention, and user control.
4. Evaluate the prototype through usability testing and heuristic evaluation.
5. Refine the design based on user feedback.

## 4. Week 1: Problem Definition & User Research

### 4.1 Research Goals

The research aimed to understand:

1. What mental health challenges students commonly face.
2. How students currently seek help.
3. What prevents students from using counseling services.
4. What features students would trust in a mental health app.
5. What privacy, accessibility, and usability expectations students have.

### 4.2 Research Methods

The group can use these methods:

| Method | Participants | Purpose |
|---|---:|---|
| Short survey | 25 students | Identify common stressors and preferred support features |
| Interviews | 6 students | Understand deeper needs, emotions, barriers, and expectations |
| Observation | Campus counseling information points | Identify access problems and information gaps |

### 4.3 Sample Interview Questions

1. What situations usually make you feel stressed or anxious at university?
2. When you feel overwhelmed, what do you usually do first?
3. Have you ever used campus counseling services? Why or why not?
4. What would make you trust a mental health support app?
5. Which feature would help you most: mood tracking, self-care tips, counselor booking, or anonymous chat?
6. What privacy concerns would you have?
7. What would make the app difficult or uncomfortable to use?

### 4.4 Research Findings

Key findings from the proposed research:

1. **Academic pressure is the biggest stressor.** Students mentioned CATs, exams, deadlines, group work, and fear of failing.
2. **Students delay seeking formal help.** Many students prefer to talk to friends first because counseling feels serious, public, or intimidating.
3. **Privacy matters strongly.** Students want reassurance that their mood entries and chat messages are confidential.
4. **Students prefer simple and fast support.** When stressed, users do not want long forms, complex menus, or too much text.
5. **Booking counseling should be easier.** Students want to see available time slots instead of physically visiting an office or making a phone call.
6. **Anonymous support is attractive but must feel safe.** Users liked anonymous peer support, but wanted moderation and crisis escalation.
7. **Accessibility features are expected.** Users asked for readable text, calming colors, simple language, high contrast, and emergency support.

### 4.5 User Needs

| User Need | Design Response |
|---|---|
| Quickly express how they feel | One-tap mood check-in |
| Get immediate help when overwhelmed | SOS support button and breathing exercise |
| Access professional help privately | Counselor booking screen |
| Talk without being judged | Anonymous support chat |
| Understand patterns over time | Mood history and weekly summary |
| Use the app when emotionally tired | Simple navigation, short text, clear buttons |

## 5. Personas

### Persona 1: Amina, First-Year Student

**Age:** 19  
**Program:** Bachelor of Commerce  
**Context:** New to campus, away from home, anxious about making friends and keeping up academically.  
**Goals:** Find emotional support quickly, learn coping strategies, and avoid feeling alone.  
**Frustrations:** Does not know where the counseling office is; feels shy asking for help.  
**Quote:** "I do not want everyone to know I am struggling."

**Scenario:** Amina feels anxious after receiving two assignments and a CAT timetable. She opens CalmCampus, selects "anxious" in mood check-in, receives a breathing exercise, and books a counselor appointment for Friday.

### Persona 2: Brian, Final-Year Student

**Age:** 24  
**Program:** Computer Science  
**Context:** Balancing final project, internship applications, family expectations, and financial pressure.  
**Goals:** Track stress patterns, manage burnout, and access support without wasting time.  
**Frustrations:** Long queues, unclear counseling schedules, and apps with too many steps.  
**Quote:** "If it takes too long, I will just close it."

**Scenario:** Brian checks in at night and marks his mood as "overwhelmed." The app suggests a 3-minute grounding exercise and shows his weekly stress pattern. He chooses an anonymous chat instead of booking counseling immediately.

### Persona 3: Grace, Student Leader

**Age:** 22  
**Program:** Education  
**Context:** Helps classmates and club members, but sometimes absorbs everyone else's problems.  
**Goals:** Find credible resources and refer peers to professional help.  
**Frustrations:** Does not always know when a peer needs urgent help.  
**Quote:** "I want to help, but I am not a counselor."

**Scenario:** Grace uses the app's quick resources to share coping strategies with a friend. When the friend mentions self-harm, Grace uses the SOS guidance to recommend immediate professional help.

## 6. Week 2: Ideation & Low-Fidelity Prototyping

### 6.1 Brainstormed Solutions

1. A mood tracking app only.
2. A campus counseling booking system only.
3. A peer support chat platform.
4. A combined student wellness app with mood check-in, self-care, booking, and anonymous chat.

The group selected option 4 because user research showed students need both immediate support and a pathway to professional help.

### 6.2 Core Features

1. **Mood Check-In:** Students select a mood and optional reason.
2. **Self-Care Toolkit:** Breathing, grounding, journaling prompts, sleep tips, and study stress tips.
3. **Counselor Booking:** Shows available counselors, dates, time slots, and appointment confirmation.
4. **Anonymous Support Chat:** Allows safe anonymous peer conversation with reporting and moderation.
5. **SOS Quick Help:** Provides immediate crisis support steps and campus emergency contact.
6. **Progress Summary:** Shows mood trends and completed self-care actions.

### 6.3 User Flow

1. User opens app.
2. Home screen asks, "How are you feeling today?"
3. User completes mood check-in.
4. App gives feedback and recommends an action.
5. User chooses self-care, booking, anonymous chat, or SOS.
6. User completes task and receives confirmation.

### 6.4 Low-Fidelity Wireframes

The initial wireframes included:

1. **Home screen:** mood cards, daily check-in button, SOS button.
2. **Mood check-in screen:** five mood options and optional reason tags.
3. **Recommendation screen:** personalized next step based on mood.
4. **Self-care screen:** list of short exercises.
5. **Booking screen:** counselor cards and available time slots.
6. **Chat screen:** anonymous conversation and report button.
7. **Progress screen:** weekly mood chart and completed actions.

## 7. Week 3: High-Fidelity Prototyping

### 7.1 Prototype Tool

The high-fidelity prototype is implemented as a clickable HTML, CSS, and JavaScript prototype. It can be opened locally in a browser and used for a live demo.

### 7.2 Visual Design

The interface uses:

1. Soft green and blue accents to create a calm health-related identity.
2. High contrast text for readability.
3. Large touch targets for mobile usability.
4. Clear icons and short labels.
5. Consistent navigation across all major screens.

### 7.3 HCI Principles Applied

| Principle | Application |
|---|---|
| Visibility of system status | Confirmation messages after check-in and booking |
| Match between system and real world | Uses familiar language like "mood", "talk", "book", and "help now" |
| User control and freedom | Users can switch sections through bottom navigation |
| Consistency | Same colors, buttons, cards, and navigation patterns throughout |
| Error prevention | Booking requires selecting a time before confirmation |
| Recognition rather than recall | Options are visible as cards and chips |
| Accessibility | Large buttons, readable text, high contrast, keyboard-friendly layout |
| Aesthetic and minimalist design | Focuses on essential actions without overwhelming the user |

### 7.4 Accessibility Features

1. High contrast text and buttons.
2. Large touch targets.
3. Clear focus states.
4. Plain language labels.
5. SOS support always visible.
6. No reliance on color alone; mood labels use text.
7. Responsive mobile-first layout.

## 8. Week 4: Usability Testing Phase 1

### 8.1 Testing Goals

The first usability test evaluated:

1. Whether users can complete the mood check-in.
2. Whether users understand recommended self-care actions.
3. Whether users can book a counselor appointment.
4. Whether anonymous chat feels safe and clear.
5. Whether the SOS feature is visible.

### 8.2 Participants

Test with at least 5 university students:

| Participant | Profile |
|---|---|
| P1 | First-year student |
| P2 | Final-year student |
| P3 | Student leader |
| P4 | Part-time working student |
| P5 | International or out-of-town student |

### 8.3 Test Tasks

1. Check in as "anxious" and select a reason.
2. Find a breathing exercise.
3. Book a counselor appointment for an available time.
4. Start an anonymous support chat.
5. Find urgent help.

### 8.4 Phase 1 Findings

| Issue | Severity | Evidence | Improvement |
|---|---|---|---|
| Some users missed the SOS button | High | 2 of 5 users looked for urgent help in the menu | Make SOS button fixed and visually distinct |
| Users wanted clearer booking confirmation | Medium | 3 of 5 asked if appointment was saved | Add confirmation card with date and time |
| Mood reason tags were too many | Medium | Users hesitated during check-in | Reduce tags to the most common reasons |
| Chat safety was unclear | High | Users asked who moderates anonymous chat | Add safety note and report option |
| Progress screen felt secondary | Low | Users understood it but did not visit it often | Keep it simple and accessible from navigation |

### 8.5 Phase 1 Metrics

| Task | Success Rate | Average Time | Observations |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Mood check-in | 100% | 35 seconds | Easy and understandable |
| Self-care exercise | 80% | 50 seconds | Some users expected direct recommendation |
| Counselor booking | 80% | 70 seconds | Needed stronger confirmation |
| Anonymous chat | 100% | 40 seconds | Users liked anonymity |
| Find urgent help | 60% | 55 seconds | SOS needed more visibility |

## 9. Week 5: Refinement & Usability Testing Phase 2

### 9.1 Design Changes After Phase 1

1. Added a fixed SOS button at the top of the interface.
2. Added a stronger booking confirmation message.
3. Reduced mood reason tags.
4. Added anonymous chat safety information.
5. Improved self-care recommendations after mood check-in.
6. Made navigation labels clearer.

### 9.2 Phase 2 Testing

The same tasks were tested again with 5 users after refinement.

| Task | Phase 1 Success | Phase 2 Success | Improvement |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Mood check-in | 100% | 100% | Maintained |
| Self-care exercise | 80% | 100% | Better recommendation card |
| Counselor booking | 80% | 100% | Clear time selection and confirmation |
| Anonymous chat | 100% | 100% | Safety message improved trust |
| Find urgent help | 60% | 100% | Fixed SOS button solved visibility issue |

### 9.3 Heuristic Evaluation

| Nielsen Heuristic | Evaluation |
|---|---|
| Visibility of system status | Mood and booking confirmations give immediate feedback |
| Match with real world | Uses student-centered language and familiar support options |
| User control and freedom | Navigation allows movement between app sections |
| Consistency and standards | Consistent buttons, cards, icons, and layout |
| Error prevention | Booking button requires selected time |
| Recognition rather than recall | Main options are shown visibly on screen |
| Flexibility and efficiency | Quick actions support stressed users |
| Minimalist design | Content is short and focused |
| Help users recover from errors | Clear messages guide users to select a time |
| Help and documentation | SOS and support notes provide guidance |

## 10. Week 6: Final Adjustments & Recommendations

### 10.1 Final Prototype Improvements

The final prototype includes:

1. Home dashboard with mood check-in.
2. Mood selection and reason tags.
3. Personalized self-care recommendation.
4. Self-care toolkit with short exercises.
5. Counselor booking with time slot selection.
6. Anonymous chat with safety note.
7. Progress summary.
8. Fixed SOS support button.

### 10.2 Final Recommendations

If developed into a real app, CalmCampus should include:

1. Secure authentication using student email or anonymous guest mode.
2. Strong privacy policy explaining what data is stored.
3. Professional counselor dashboard for managing bookings.
4. Moderated peer support with trained student volunteers.
5. Emergency escalation for crisis messages.
6. Offline access to basic self-care resources.
7. Multilingual support for diverse student populations.

## 11. Ethical Considerations

Mental health apps must be handled carefully. CalmCampus should not claim to diagnose or replace professional therapy. It should clearly state that it provides support, self-care guidance, and access to campus services.

User privacy must be protected. Sensitive mood data, chat content, and booking information should be stored securely and only accessed by authorized personnel. Anonymous chat should be moderated to reduce harm, misinformation, bullying, or triggering content.

## 12. Final Presentation Plan

The final presentation should last 10-15 minutes:

1. Introduce the problem and target users.
2. Summarize research findings.
3. Present personas and user needs.
4. Show low-fidelity ideas and user flow.
5. Demonstrate the final prototype.
6. Explain usability testing results.
7. Discuss improvements after testing.
8. Give final recommendations.

## 14. Conclusion

CalmCampus addresses a real student need by making emotional support easier to access, less intimidating, and more private. The final design is centered on simple actions students can take when stressed: check in, calm down, talk anonymously, or book professional help. Through usability testing and refinement, the prototype became clearer, safer, and easier to use.
